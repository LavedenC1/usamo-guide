## Overview

The Lifting the Exponent Lemma, often abbreviated as LTE, is a powerful tool in
olympiad number theory.

It helps determine how many times a prime divides expressions such as

$$
a^n-b^n
$$

or

$$
a^n+b^n.
$$

In other words, LTE is useful when we want to compute the exponent of a prime
inside a number.

For example, instead of just asking whether $3$ divides $10^6-1$, we can ask:

> What is the largest power of $3$ that divides $10^6-1$?

LTE gives a fast and systematic way to answer questions like this.

## Motivation

Consider

$$
10^6-1.
$$

We can factor it as

$$
10^6-1=(10^3-1)(10^3+1),
$$

or even further using difference of powers. However if we only care about
powers of $3$, expanding or fully factoring the expression is inefficient.

Instead notice that

$$
10 \equiv 1 \pmod{3},
$$

so $3$ divides $10-1$.

LTE tells us that under suitable conditions,

$$
v_3(10^6-1)=v_3(10-1)+v_3(6).
$$

Since

$$
v_3(9)=2
$$

and

$$
v_3(6)=1,
$$

we get

$$
v_3(10^6-1)=3.
$$

So $3^3$ divides $10^6-1$, but $3^4$ does not.

This kind of computation appears frequently in olympiad divisibility problems.

## Prime Valuation

Before stating LTE, we need one piece of notation.

For a prime $p$ and a positive integer $N$, define

$$
v_p(N)
$$

to be the exponent of $p$ in the prime factorisation of $N$.

In other words, $v_p(N)$ is the largest integer $k$ such that

$$
p^k \mid N.
$$

### Examples

Since

$$
72=2^3 \times 3^2,
$$

we have

$$
v_2(72)=3
$$

and

$$
v_3(72)=2.
$$

Also,

$$
v_5(72)=0,
$$

because $5$ does not divide $72$.

This notation allows us to discuss divisibility by prime powers far more simply.

## Main Statement: Difference Form

The most common version of LTE is the difference form.

### Lifting the Exponent Lemma

Let $p$ be an odd prime. Suppose that $p\mid a-b$ and $p\nmid ab$. Then for
every positive integer $n$,

$$
v_p(a^n-b^n)
=
v_p(a-b)+v_p(n).
$$

This is the version used most often in olympiad problems.

## Why the Conditions Matter

The assumptions are important.

- We require $p$ to be an odd prime.
- We require $p\mid a-b$.
- We require $p\nmid ab$.

The condition $p\mid a-b$ means that

$$
a\equiv b \pmod p.
$$

The condition $p\nmid ab$ means that neither $a$ nor $b$ is divisible by $p$.
This prevents degenerate cases where both powers contain extra factors of $p$
for unrelated reasons.

## Intuition Behind LTE

The expression

$$
a^n-b^n
$$

always factors as

$$
(a-b)(a^{n-1}+a^{n-2}b+\cdots+ab^{n-2}+b^{n-1}).
$$

So $a-b$ is always a factor.

LTE says something more significant. If an odd prime $p$ divides $a-b$, then the
additional powers of $p$ that appear in $a^n-b^n$ come from the exponent
$n$.

That is why the formula is

$$
v_p(a^n-b^n)
=
v_p(a-b)+v_p(n).
$$

The first term comes from the base difference $a-b$, and the second term comes
from the exponent $n$.

## Proof Sketch

We can outline the idea behind the proof for odd primes.

Write

$$
a=b+h,
$$

where $p\mid h$.

Then

$$
a^n-b^n=(b+h)^n-b^n.
$$

Using the binomial theorem,

$$
(b+h)^n-b^n
=
nb^{n-1}h+\binom{n}{2}b^{n-2}h^2+\cdots+h^n.
$$

The first term is

$$
nb^{n-1}h.
$$

Since $p\nmid b$, the $p$-adic contribution of this term is essentially

$$
v_p(n)+v_p(h).
$$

The later terms contain higher powers of $h$, so they are more divisible by
$p$. Under the hypotheses of LTE, these later terms do not change the final
valuation.

Since

$$
h=a-b,
$$

this gives

$$
v_p(a^n-b^n)=v_p(a-b)+v_p(n).
$$

This proof sketch explains why the formula has the shape it does. A fully
rigorous proof requires checking that the later binomial terms have large enough $p$-adic valuation.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1

> Find the largest power of $3$ dividing $10^6-1$.

We use LTE with

$$
p=3,\qquad a=10,\qquad b=1,\qquad n=6.
$$

Since

$$
3\mid 10-1
$$

and

$$
3\nmid 10,
$$

the conditions are satisfied.

Therefore,

$$
v_3(10^6-1)=v_3(10-1)+v_3(6).
$$

Now

$$
v_3(10-1)=v_3(9)=2
$$

and

$$
v_3(6)=1.
$$

Thus

$$
v_3(10^6-1)=3.
$$

So the largest power of $3$ dividing $10^6-1$ is $3^3$.
