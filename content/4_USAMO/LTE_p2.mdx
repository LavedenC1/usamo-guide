### Example 2

> Find $v_5(11^{20}-1)$.

We apply LTE with

$$
p=5,\qquad a=11,\qquad b=1,\qquad n=20.
$$

Since

$$
5\mid 11-1
$$

and

$$
5\nmid 11,
$$

LTE gives

$$
v_5(11^{20}-1)=v_5(11-1)+v_5(20).
$$

Now

$$
v_5(11-1)=v_5(10)=1
$$

and

$$
v_5(20)=1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
v_5(11^{20}-1)=2.
$$

So $25$ divides $11^{20}-1$, but $125$ does not.

### Example 3

> Find $v_7(15^{14}-1)$.

Use LTE with

$$
p=7,\qquad a=15,\qquad b=1,\qquad n=14.
$$

Since

$$
15\equiv 1 \pmod 7,
$$

we have

$$
7\mid 15-1.
$$

Also $7\nmid 15$, so the conditions hold.

As such

$$
v_7(15^{14}-1)=v_7(15-1)+v_7(14).
$$

Now both $15-1$ and $14$ contain exactly one factor of $7$, so

$$
v_7(15-1)=1
$$

and

$$
v_7(14)=1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
v_7(15^{14}-1)=2.
$$

So the largest power of $7$ dividing $15^{14}-1$ is $7^2$.

## Sum Form

There is also a useful sum version of LTE.

Let $p$ be an odd prime. Suppose that

$$
p\mid a+b
$$

and

$$
p\nmid ab.
$$

If $n$ is odd, then

$$
v_p(a^n+b^n)
=
v_p(a+b)+v_p(n).
$$

This follows by applying the difference form to

$$
a^n-(-b)^n.
$$

When $n$ is odd,

$$
(-b)^n=-b^n,
$$

so

$$
a^n-(-b)^n=a^n+b^n.
$$

## Worked Examples for the Sum Form

### Example 4

> Find $v_3(8^9+1)$.

We use the sum form with

$$
p=3,\qquad a=8,\qquad b=1,\qquad n=9.
$$

Since

$$
3\mid 8+1
$$

and $n$ is odd, LTE gives

$$
v_3(8^9+1)=v_3(8+1)+v_3(9).
$$

Now both $8+1$ and $9$ contain exactly two factors of $3$, so

$$
v_3(8+1)=2
$$

and

$$
v_3(9)=2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
v_3(8^9+1)=4.
$$

So the largest power of $3$ dividing $8^9+1$ is $3^4$.

### Example 5

> Find $v_5(4^{15}+1)$.

Since

$$
5\mid 4+1
$$

and $15$ is odd, we may apply the sum form:

$$
v_5(4^{15}+1)=v_5(4+1)+v_5(15).
$$

Now

$$
v_5(4+1)=v_5(5)=1
$$

and

$$
v_5(15)=1.
$$

Thus

$$
v_5(4^{15}+1)=2.
$$

So $25$ divides $4^{15}+1$, but $125$ does not.

## The Case $p=2$

The prime $2$ requires special care. The simplest useful version is the
following.

If $a$ and $b$ are odd positive integers and $n$ is a positive integer, then

### If $n$ is odd

$$
v_2(a^n-b^n)=v_2(a-b).
$$

### If $n$ is even

$$
v_2(a^n-b^n)
=
v_2(a-b)+v_2(a+b)+v_2(n)-1.
$$

This version is very useful, but it is also easier to misuse. Always check that
$a$ and $b$ are odd before applying it.

## Worked Examples for $p=2$

### Example 6

> Find $v_2(5^{12}-1)$.

Here

$$
a=5,\qquad b=1,\qquad n=12.
$$

Since $a$ and $b$ are odd and $n$ is even, we use the even version:

$$
v_2(5^{12}-1)
=
v_2(5-1)+v_2(5+1)+v_2(12)-1.
$$

Now

$$
v_2(5-1)=v_2(4)=2,
$$

$$
v_2(5+1)=v_2(6)=1,
$$

and

$$
v_2(12)=2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
v_2(5^{12}-1)=2+1+2-1=4.
$$

So the largest power of $2$ dividing $5^{12}-1$ is $2^4$.

### Example 7

> Find $v_2(7^9-1)$.

Here

$$
a=7,\qquad b=1,\qquad n=9.
$$

Since $a$ and $b$ are odd and $n$ is odd,

$$
v_2(7^9-1)=v_2(7-1).
$$

Thus

$$
v_2(7^9-1)=v_2(6)=1.
$$

So the largest power of $2$ dividing $7^9-1$ is $2$.

## Using LTE in Olympiad Problems

LTE is most useful when a problem asks for:
- The highest power of a prime dividing an expression
- Divisibility by a large prime power
- Solving equations involving expressions like $a^n-b^n$
- Comparing valuations on both sides of an equation
- Proving that an expression is not a perfect power

A common pattern is to apply $v_p$ to both sides of an equation.

For example, if a problem gives

$$
a^n-b^n=p^k,
$$

then applying LTE can turn the equation into a much simpler statement about

$$
v_p(a-b)+v_p(n).
$$

## Key Contest Insight

When you see an expression like

$$
a^n-b^n
$$

or

$$
a^n+b^n,
$$

and a prime divides $a-b$ or $a+b$, consider using LTE.

The key question is not just whether the prime divides the expression, but how
many times it divides the expression.

LTE converts a difficult divisibility question into a simple valuation formula.

## Strategy Tips

- Check whether the expression is of the form $a^n-b^n$ or $a^n+b^n$.
- Identify the prime $p$ whose power you want to count.
- Check whether $p\mid a-b$ for the difference form.
- Check whether $p\mid a+b$ and $n$ is odd for the sum form.
- Make sure $p\nmid ab$.
- Treat $p=2$ separately.
- After applying LTE, compute each valuation carefully.
- In equation problems, take $v_p$ of both sides.

## Common Pitfalls

- Applying the odd prime version when $p=2$.
- Forgetting to check that $p\nmid ab$.
- Using the sum form when $n$ is even.
- Confusing $v_p(a-b)$ with $a-b$ itself.
- Forgetting that $v_p(n)$ contributes extra powers of $p$.
- Applying LTE before checking that $p$ divides $a-b$ or $a+b$.
