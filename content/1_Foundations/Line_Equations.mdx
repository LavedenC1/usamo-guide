## Overview

Lines are the simplest coordinate objects. Many geometry problems reduce to finding a line and intersecting it with another curve. This module covers line representations, switching between forms, and problem‑solving techniques (perpendicular bisectors, vertical/horizontal cases, intersections).

A line is determined by:
- two points, or
- one point and a slope (including zero or undefined slope).

## Key Ideas

- **Point‑slope:** $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ – use when you know a point and slope.  
  Example: $(2,3)$ with slope $4$ → $y-3 = 4(x-2)$.

- **Slope‑intercept:** $y = mx + b$ – use when you know slope $m$ and / or $y$-intercept $b$.  
  Example: $y = -2x + 5$ has slope $-2$, intercept $(0,5)$.

- **Standard form:** $ax + by = c$ (this form is less common, but still useful to know)
  Example: $3x - 2y = 7$ → convert to $y = \frac{3}{2}x - \frac{7}{2}$.

- **Perpendicular slopes:** $m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1$ (negative reciprocal).  
  Horizontal ($m=0$) ⟂ vertical (undefined slope).

- **Horizontal lines:** $y = c$ (slope $0$).  
- **Vertical lines:** $x = c$ (slope undefined).

## Core Skills
- Recognizing when you have enough information to use one of the various slope equations
- Recognizing when you can use lines to aid with geometry (this is known as coordbashing)
- Setting variables for unknown values within a line equation
## Worked Example
Line $l$ passes through the points $(-1,2)$ and $(5,m)$, where $m$ is a real number. If $l$ has a slope of $3$, find $m$.
Putting our equation into point-slope form, we obtain the following:
$y-2 = 3(x+1)$
Converting into slope-intercept form, we get the following:
$y=3x+5$
Plugging in $5$ for $x$, we get that $m = 20$.

Alternatively, you could have noticed that the difference between the $x$ values of the first and second point are $6$, implying that $m = 2 + 3(6) = 20$.

## More Examples

### Example 1: Geometry

$\triangle ABC$ has coordinates $A, B, C$, which are located at $(0,0)$, $(8,0)$, and $(4,3)$ respectively.  
If the perpendicular bisector of $\overline{AB}$ passes through the line $y = -5x + 2$ at the ordered pair $(m,n)$, compute $m + n$.

First, find the perpendicular bisector of $\overline{AB}$. The midpoint of $AB$ is $(4,0)$. Since $A$ and $B$ lie on a horizontal line ($y=0$), the perpendicular bisector is vertical: $x = 4$.  
To find where $x = 4$ intersects $y = -5x + 2$, plug in $x=4$:  
$y = -5(4) + 2 = -18$.  
The ordered pair is $(4,-18)$, so $m + n = 4 + (-18) = -14$.

### Example 2: Perpendicularity

If line segment $\overline{AB}$ has endpoints $A(0,8)$ and $B(8,0)$, find the equation of the perpendicular bisector of $\overline{AB}$.

Midpoint of $\overline{AB}$ is $(4,4)$.  
Slope of $\overline{AB}$ is $\frac{0-8}{8-0} = -1$, so the perpendicular slope is $1$.  
Using point-slope form: $y - 4 = 1(x - 4)$ → $y = x$.

### Example 3: Vertical Line

Find the equation of the line through $(2,-3)$ with undefined slope.

Undefined slope means a vertical line: $x = 2$.

### Example 4: Intersection of Perpendicular Bisector with Another Line

Triangle $\triangle ABC$ has vertices $A(1,2)$, $B(7,6)$, and $C(3,8)$.  
Find the equation of the perpendicular bisector of $\overline{AB}$.  
Then determine the intersection point of this perpendicular bisector with the line $y = \frac{1}{2}x - 4$, and compute the sum of the coordinates of the intersection point.

Midpoint of $AB$: $\left(\frac{1+7}{2}, \frac{2+6}{2}\right) = (4,4)$.  
Slope of $AB$: $\frac{6-2}{7-1} = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3}$.  
Perpendicular slope: $-\frac{3}{2}$.  
Equation: $y - 4 = -\frac{3}{2}(x - 4)$ → $y = -\frac{3}{2}x + 10$.  
Intersect with $y = \frac{1}{2}x - 4$:  
$-\frac{3}{2}x + 10 = \frac{1}{2}x - 4$ → multiply by 2: $-3x + 20 = x - 8$ → $-4x = -28$ → $x = 7$.  
Then $y = \frac{1}{2}(7) - 4 = -0.5$.  
Sum: $7 + (-0.5) = 6.5$.

### Example 5: Finding an Endpoint from the Perpendicular Bisector

The perpendicular bisector of segment $\overline{PQ}$ is given by $2x - y = 5$.  
If $P = (2,1)$, find the coordinates of $Q$.

Rewrite bisector as $y = 2x - 5$, so slope m_bis = 2.
So slope of $\overline{PQ}$ must be $-\frac{1}{2}$ (negative reciprocal).  
Let $Q = (x,y)$. Slope condition: $\frac{y-1}{x-2} = -\frac{1}{2}$ → $2(y-1) = -(x-2)$ → $x + 2y = 4$. (1)  
Midpoint $M = \left(\frac{x+2}{2}, \frac{y+1}{2}\right)$ lies on bisector: $2\left(\frac{x+2}{2}\right) - \frac{y+1}{2} = 5$ → $(x+2) - \frac{y+1}{2} = 5$.  
Multiply by 2: $2x+4 - (y+1) = 10$ → $2x - y = 7$. (2)  
Solve (1) and (2): From (1), $x = 4 - 2y$. Substitute into (2): $2(4-2y) - y = 7$ → $8 - 4y - y = 7$ → $8 -5y = 7$ → $y = \frac{1}{5}$.  
Then $x = 4 - 2\cdot\frac{1}{5} = \frac{18}{5}$. Thus $Q = \left(\frac{18}{5}, \frac{1}{5}\right)$.

### Example 6: Perpendicular Bisector Crossing a Vertical Line

Find the equation of the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining $(2,5)$ and $(8,3)$.  
Then determine the point where this perpendicular bisector crosses the vertical line $x = 5$, and give the $y$-coordinate.

Midpoint: $\left(\frac{2+8}{2}, \frac{5+3}{2}\right) = (5,4)$.  
Slope of segment: $\frac{3-5}{8-2} = \frac{-2}{6} = -\frac{1}{3}$.  
Perpendicular slope: $3$.  
Equation: $y - 4 = 3(x - 5)$ → $y = 3x - 11$.  
Intersect with $x = 5$: $y = 3(5) - 11 = 4$.  
Point: $(5,4)$ → $y$-coordinate is $4$.


## Strategy Checklist

- Compute the slope of the original segment unless the segment is vertical or horizontal.
- Determine the midpoint correctly: average of $x$-coordinates and $y$-coordinates.
- For a perpendicular bisector: slope is the **negative reciprocal** of the original slope.  
- If original slope is $0$ (horizontal) → perpendicular bisector is vertical (undefined slope).  
- If original slope is undefined (vertical) → perpendicular bisector is horizontal (slope $0$).
- Use point-slope form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ when you know a point and the slope.
- Simplify equations to slope-intercept form $y = mx + b$ or standard form as needed.
- To find an intersection, substitute one equation into the other or solve the system.
- Check if the line is vertical or horizontal before writing an equation (avoid undefined slope errors).
- Always verify that your computed intersection satisfies both original equations.
- When an endpoint is unknown, set up equations using the midpoint formula and the perpendicular slope condition.

## Common Pitfalls

- Mixing up the negative reciprocal: forgetting the negative sign or taking the reciprocal only.
- Using the original slope instead of the perpendicular slope for the bisector.
- Dropping a sign when distributing in point-slope form, especially with negative fractions.
- Forgetting that a perpendicular bisector must pass through the midpoint (not just be perpendicular).
- Using vertical lines in slope form (slope undefined).
- Arithmetic errors when averaging coordinates or solving linear equations with fractions.
- Assuming the perpendicular bisector is unique: it always is, but be careful with degenerate cases (segment length zero).

## Overview

Lines are the simplest coordinate objects, yet mastery of their properties unlocks a vast range of competition problems. Many geometry problems reduce to finding a line and intersecting it with another curve. This module covers every line representation, how to switch between them, and a full suite of problem-solving techniques — from perpendicular bisectors and angle bisectors to locus problems, parametric form, and coordbashing strategies used at the AMC 12 and AIME level.

A line is determined by **exactly two independent pieces of information**, such as:
- Two distinct points, or
- One point and a slope (including zero or undefined slope), or
- Two intercepts (provided neither is the origin for the intercept form), or
- A normal direction and a signed distance from the origin.

## Key Formulas at a Glance

| Form | Equation | Best used when… |
|---|---|---|
| Point-slope | $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ | You know a point and slope |
| Slope-intercept | $y = mx + b$ | You know slope and $y$-intercept |
| Standard / General | $ax + by = c$ | Checking parallel/perpendicular families |
| Two-point | $\frac{y-y_1}{x-x_1} = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$ | You know two points |
| Intercept | $\frac{x}{a}+\frac{y}{b}=1$ | You know both intercepts |
| Normal | $x\cos\alpha + y\sin\alpha = p$ | Distance from origin is $p$ |
| Parametric | $x=h+t\cos\theta,\ y=k+t\sin\theta$ | Walking along a line at angle $\theta$ |

$$ax + by = c \qquad (a,b \text{ not both zero})$$

### 1.4 Two-Point Form

Given $(x_1,y_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2)$:

$$y - y_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}(x - x_1)$$

Equivalently, the **collinearity determinant**:

$$\begin{vmatrix} x & y & 1 \\ x_1 & y_1 & 1 \\ x_2 & y_2 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = 0$$

This determinant form is clean for **collinearity checks** — expand it to get the two-point line equation directly.

### 1.5 Intercept Form

$$\frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} = 1$$

where $a$ is the $x$-intercept and $b$ is the $y$-intercept. The area of the triangle cut off with the axes is $\tfrac{1}{2}|ab|$.

## 2. Slope: The Central Quantity

For any two points $(x_1,y_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2)$ with $x_1 \neq x_2$:

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$$

- **Horizontal line** ($y = c$): $m = 0$.
- **Vertical line** ($x = c$): slope undefined.
- **Parallel lines:** equal slopes, $m_1 = m_2$.
- **Perpendicular lines:** $m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1$ (negative reciprocal). Exception: a horizontal line is perpendicular to a vertical line.

> **Common Pitfall:** Forgetting the negative sign in the negative reciprocal. The perpendicular to slope $\frac{3}{4}$ is $-\frac{4}{3}$, not $\frac{4}{3}$.

### Slope and Angle

If a line makes angle $\theta$ with the positive $x$-axis, then $m = \tan\theta$.

The **angle between two lines** with slopes $m_1$ and $m_2$ satisfies:

$$\tan\phi = \left|\frac{m_1 - m_2}{1 + m_1 m_2}\right|$$

If $1 + m_1 m_2 = 0$, the lines are perpendicular.

### 3.1 Distance from a Point to a Line

The distance from point $(x_0, y_0)$ to the line $ax + by + c = 0$ is:

$$d = \frac{|ax_0 + by_0 + c|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}}$$

### 3.2 Distance Between Parallel Lines

For $ax + by + c_1 = 0$ and $ax + by + c_2 = 0$ (same $a, b$):

$$d = \frac{|c_1 - c_2|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}}$$

## 4. Parametric Form

The line through $(h,k)$ making angle $\theta$ with the $x$-axis:

$$\frac{x-h}{\cos\theta} = \frac{y-k}{\sin\theta} = t$$

So $x = h + t\cos\theta$, $y = k + t\sin\theta$. Here $|t|$ is the distance from $(h,k)$ to the point $(x,y)$.

**Why this matters for competitions:** If a line through $P$ meets a curve at points $A$ and $B$, then $PA = |t_1|$ and $PB = |t_2|$. The values $t_1, t_2$ are roots of the equation you get after substituting the parametric form into the curve's equation, so:

$$PA + PB = |t_1 + t_2|, \quad PA \cdot PB = |t_1 t_2|$$

which you can read off via **Vieta's formulas** — no coordinates needed.

## 5. Perpendicular Bisectors and Triangle Centers

### 5.1 Perpendicular Bisector

The perpendicular bisector of segment $\overline{AB}$ is the locus of points equidistant from $A$ and $B$. To find it:

1. Compute the midpoint $M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)$.
2. Compute slope of $\overline{AB}$: $m = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$.
3. Perpendicular slope: $m_\perp = -1/m$.
4. Line through $M$ with slope $m_\perp$.

## 6. Area via Coordinates

### 6.1 Triangle Area (Shoelace Formula)

$$[ABC] = \frac{1}{2}\left|x_1(y_2-y_3) + x_2(y_3-y_1) + x_3(y_1-y_2)\right|$$

### 6.2 Polygon Area (General Shoelace)

For a polygon with vertices $(x_1,y_1), \ldots, (x_n,y_n)$ listed in order:

$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}\left|\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i y_{i+1} - x_{i+1} y_i)\right|$$

(indices mod $n$).

## 7. Collinearity and Concurrency

### 7.1 Collinearity (Three Points)

Three points $A$, $B$, $C$ are collinear iff **any one** of:
- $[ABC] = 0$ (shoelace gives zero),
- Slope of $AB$ equals slope of $AC$,
- $C$ satisfies the equation of line $AB$.

### 7.2 Concurrency (Three Lines)

Three lines $\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell_3$ are concurrent iff:

$$\begin{vmatrix} a_1 & b_1 & c_1\\ a_2 & b_2 & c_2\\ a_3 & b_3 & c_3 \end{vmatrix} = 0$$

where each line is $a_i x + b_i y + c_i = 0$.

> **AMC Tip:** The family of lines through the intersection of $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ can be written as $\ell_1 + \lambda \ell_2 = 0$. This avoids finding the intersection point explicitly and is powerful for locus problems.

### 8.2 Foot of Perpendicular

The foot of the perpendicular from $P(x_0,y_0)$ to line $ax+by+c=0$:

$$F = \left(x_0 - \frac{a(ax_0+by_0+c)}{a^2+b^2},\ y_0 - \frac{b(ax_0+by_0+c)}{a^2+b^2}\right)$$

### 8.3 Angle Bisectors

The locus of points equidistant from two lines is:

$$\frac{a_1x+b_1y+c_1}{\sqrt{a_1^2+b_1^2}} = \pm\frac{a_2x+b_2y+c_2}{\sqrt{a_2^2+b_2^2}}$$

The two signs give the **internal** and **external** angle bisectors, which are perpendicular to each other.

### 8.4 Ceva and Menelaus

**Menelaus' Theorem:** Points $D$ on $BC$, $E$ on $CA$, $F$ on $AB$ are collinear iff:
$$\frac{BD}{DC}\cdot\frac{CE}{EA}\cdot\frac{AF}{FB} = -1 \quad (\text{signed ratios})$$

**Ceva's Theorem:** Cevians $AD$, $BE$, $CF$ are concurrent iff:
$$\frac{BD}{DC}\cdot\frac{CE}{EA}\cdot\frac{AF}{FB} = +1 \quad (\text{signed ratios})$$

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Foundational

Line $\ell$ passes through $(-1,2)$ and $(5,m)$ with slope $3$. Find $m$.

**Solution:** $\Delta x = 6$, so $\Delta y = 3 \cdot 6 = 18$, giving $m = 2 + 18 = \boxed{20}$.

---

### Example 2 — Area via Intercept Form (AMC Style)

A line passes through $(2,2)$ and cuts a triangle of area $9$ from the first quadrant. Find all possible slopes.

**Solution:** Write the line as $\frac{x}{a}+\frac{y}{b}=1$ with $a,b>0$. Passing through $(2,2)$: $\frac{2}{a}+\frac{2}{b}=1$. Area condition: $\frac{1}{2}ab=9 \Rightarrow ab=18$.

From the constraint: $2b+2a=ab=18 \Rightarrow a+b=9$. So $a$ and $b$ are roots of $t^2-9t+18=0$, giving $(t-3)(t-6)=0$, so $(a,b)\in\{(3,6),(6,3)\}$.

Slopes: $m=-b/a \in \{-2, -1/2\}$. Sum of slopes $= \boxed{-5/2}$.

---

### Example 3 — Parametric + Vieta

The line $y-\sqrt{3}x+3=0$ cuts the parabola $y^2=x+2$ at $A$ and $B$. Find $PA\cdot PB$ where $P=(\sqrt{3},0)$.

**Solution:** The line has slope $\sqrt{3}$, so $\theta=60°$. Parametrize from $P$:
$$x = \sqrt{3}+\tfrac{t}{2}, \quad y = \tfrac{t\sqrt{3}}{2}$$

Substituting into $y^2=x+2$:
$$\frac{3t^2}{4} = \sqrt{3}+\frac{t}{2}+2 \implies 3t^2 - 2t - 4(2+\sqrt{3})=0$$

By Vieta: $t_1 t_2 = \frac{-4(2+\sqrt{3})}{3}$, so $PA\cdot PB = |t_1 t_2| = \boxed{\dfrac{4(2+\sqrt{3})}{3}}$.

---

### Example 4 — Euler Line

In $\triangle ABC$, the centroid is $G(5/3, 3)$ and the circumcenter is $O(-9/2, 15/2)$. Find the orthocenter $H$.

**Solution:** By the Euler line relation $H = 3G - 2O$:

$$H = 3\left(\tfrac{5}{3},3\right) - 2\left(-\tfrac{9}{2},\tfrac{15}{2}\right) = (5,9) - (-9,15) = \boxed{(14,-6)}$$

## Strategy Checklist

**Setting up:**
- Identify what form of line equation the given information calls for.
- Check for vertical/horizontal lines before writing a slope (avoid undefined slope errors).
- For triangles, consider whether coordbashing or a synthetic approach is faster.

**Perpendicular bisectors:**
- Slope is the **negative reciprocal** of the original slope.
- Must pass through the **midpoint** (not just be perpendicular somewhere).

**Area problems:**
- Intercept form + $\frac{1}{2}ab=K$ is the canonical approach for first-quadrant triangle area.
- Use Vieta's formulas on the resulting quadratic for sum/product of intercepts.

**Distance problems:**
- Memorize $d = \frac{|ax_0+by_0+c|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}$.
- Distance between parallel lines: $\frac{|c_1-c_2|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}$.

**Parametric + Vieta (for chord lengths):**
- Parametrize from a base point along the line's direction angle.
- Substitute into the curve's equation; use Vieta's on the resulting polynomial.

**Reflection/shortest path:**
- Reflect one endpoint across the mirror line; verify the segment crosses the mirror.

**Euler line:**
- $H = 3G - 2O$. If any two of $H$, $G$, $O$ are known, the third is free.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Negative reciprocal:** forgetting the sign. The perpendicular to $m=2$ is $m=-1/2$, not $1/2$.
- **Bisector must pass through the midpoint.** A line perpendicular to $\overline{AB}$ is not the perpendicular bisector unless it passes through the midpoint.
- **Assuming a non-vertical slope** when the line could be vertical; check $x_1 = x_2$ first.
- **Intercept form fails** when the line passes through the origin; use point-slope instead.
- **Shoelace order matters:** vertices must be listed consistently (all CW or all CCW).
- **Parametric Vieta:** the product $t_1 t_2$ can be negative (meaning $P$ is between $A$ and $B$); take the absolute value when computing $PA \cdot PB$.
- **Euler line:** only valid for non-equilateral triangles.
