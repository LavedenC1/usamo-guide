## Overview
Geometric probability is a technique that involves using length, area, or volume to calculate probability. We can use this technique not only for problems explicitly involving geometry, but also for probability questions with **continuous** variables.

In a probability question, we want to find the ratio of the set of desired outcomes to the set of total outcomes. In a probability question with discrete variables, both sets have a finite size. However in a probability question with continuous variables, both sets have infinite size, but we need some way to compare their sizes without directly counting. This is where geometric probability can be useful. We can often convert these questions involving probability into questions involving geometry. Then, the sizes of the sets become length, areas, and volumes, which can be easier to deal with.

## Key Ideas
(To avoid repeating myself, I will simply say **area** instead of "length/area/volume".)
- In a geometric probability problem, the probability is the ratio of the area of the desired outcomes to the area of the total outcomes
- These problems essentially have two parts: (1) modelling the problem geometrically (2) calculating the areas
- As in finite probability, it can be easier to find the probability of the complement (i.e. the probability of the event **NOT** happening)

## Worked Example #1 (2011 AMC 10B #16)
A dart board is a regular octagon divided into regions as shown. Suppose that a dart thrown at the board is equally likely to land anywhere on the board. What is the probability that the dart lands within the center square?

<img src="/images/geometric-probability-0.png" width="200"/>

> ### Solution
> The probability that the dart lands within the center square is simply the ratio of the area of the center square to the area of the dart board. Since we are only concerned with the ratio between the areas, we can set the side length of the octagon to be $\sqrt{2}$ to make the calculations easier.
>
> > Remember, don't be afraid to set lengths in geometry problems to a value that will make calculations easier.
>
> <img src="/images/geometric-probability-1.png" width="300"/>
>
> The side length of the octagon and the side length of the center square are equal. So, the area of the center square is simply $(\sqrt{2})^2 = 2$.
>
> By Pythagoras, the side length of one of the right triangles on the corners of the octagon is $1$. So, the total area of the octagon is $(\sqrt{2})^2 + 4(1 \times \sqrt{2}) + 4(1 \times 1 \times \frac{1}{2}) = 2 + 4\sqrt{2} + 2 = 4 + 4\sqrt{2}$.
>
> Our answer is simply the ratio of these two numbers.
> $$\frac{2}{4+4\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{2+2\sqrt{2}} \times \frac{2-2\sqrt{2}}{2-2\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2-2\sqrt{2}}{-4} =  \boxed{\frac{\sqrt{2}-1}{2}}$$

## Worked Example #2 (1998 AIME #9)
Two mathematicians take a morning coffee break each day.  They arrive at the cafeteria independently, at random times between 9 a.m. and 10 a.m., and stay for exactly $m$ minutes.  The probability that either one arrives while the other is in the cafeteria is $40 \%,$ and $m = a - b\sqrt {c},$ where $a, b,$ and $c$ are positive integers, and $c$ is not divisible by the square of any prime.  Find $a + b + c.$
 
> ### Solution
> The problem statement doesn't directly mention geometry, so we have to make a model of the problem that involves geometry.
>
> Let $x$, $y$ be the times the two mathematicians arrive in terms of minutes past 9 am. The mathematicians meet when $|x-y|<m$. Consider plotting $x, y$ on a coordinate plane. Since the mathematicians arrive between 9 and 10 am, $0<x,y<60$. This means all pairs of arrival times are in a $60\times60$ square. The shaded region contains all pairs of arrival times where the mathematicians meet.
>
> <img src="/images/geometric-probability-2.png" width="400"/>
>
> Thus, the probability of the mathematicians meeting is equal to the ratio of the shaded area to the total area (i.e. the square). Here, it's actually more convenient to calculate the ratio of the unshaded area (two right triangles) to the total area, which is the probability that the mathematicians do not meet (which is 0.6).
>
> $\frac{(60-m)^2}{60^2} = 0.6$
> $(60-m)^2 = 36\cdot 60$
> $60 - m = 12\sqrt{15}$
> $\Rightarrow m = 60-12\sqrt{15}$
>
> So the answer is $60 + 12 + 15 = \boxed{087}$.

## Worked Example #3 (Throwing Darts!)
A normal circular dartboard has a radius of $3$. A smaller, concentric circular that we will calls the bullseye is located at the center. A dart is thrown RANDOMLY and lands uniformly somewhere inside the large circle. If you are given that the probability that the dart lands OUTSIDE the bullseye but INSIDE the large circle is 84% your job is to find the radius of the bullseye.

> ### Solution
> To start off, the probability that the dart lands in the outer ring is simply the ratio of the area of the ring to the total area of the dartboard.
>
> > Next, remember that when working with complement regions (that's what these are called), it is usually a lot easier and more efficient to calculate the area of what you *don't* want instead of what you DO want.
>
> Using this strategy, we get that the total area of the dartboard is simply $\pi \cdot 3^2 = 9\pi$. Furthermore, because the probability of the dart landing in the outer ring is $84 \%,$ that means that the probability of landing inside the bullseye is just $100\% - 84\% = 16\%.$
>
> If we let $r$ be the radius of the inner bullseye, then we can make an equation using the ratio of the bullseye area to the total area and the rest of the calculations are as follows:
>
> $\frac{\pi r^2}{9\pi} = 0.16$
>
> $\frac{r^2}{9} = \frac{16}{100}$
>
> $r^2 = \frac{144}{100}$
>
> $\Rightarrow r = \boxed{\frac{6}{5}}$

## Worked Example #4 (Illegal Corner Cutting)
A point $P$ is chosen uniformly AND also randomly inside of a square whose side lengths are of length $4$. Your job is to find the probability that the distance from $P$ to the closest vertex of the square is GREATER than $2$.

> ### Solution
> First off, notice that the problem is asking for a probability based on a distance constraint from the corners of a square, and we can easily visualize this as circular regions!
>
> So now, realize that the total area of the square is merely $4 \times 4 = 16$. Next, the region where the distance to a vertex is LESS than or equal to $2$ makes a quarter-circle that has a radius of $2$ at EACH of the $4$ corners. 
>
> Combining all this, we get that these $4$ quarter-circles form a $1$ complete circle of radius $2$. Now, the total area of this INVALID region is simply as follows:
>
> $\text{Area}_{\text{corners}} = \pi \cdot 2^2 = 4\pi$
>
> Next, subtracting this from the total area gives the VALID region in the center of the square:
>
> $\text{Area}_{\text{valid}} = 16 - 4\pi$
>
> Thus, our answer is just the ratio of the valid area to the total area, and we get our answer below!
>
> $$\frac{16 - 4\pi}{16} = \boxed{1 - \frac{\pi}{4}}$$


## Worked Example #5 (Points on a segment! (how boring))
A point $P$ is chosen uniformly and randomly on a line segment $AB$ that has length of $10$. Your job is to find the probability that the area of a square with side length of $AP$ is less than or equal to $16$.

> ### Solution
> To start off, notice that we can model the position of point $P$ CONTINUOUSLY along the line segment.
>
> Now, we will let the length of the segment $AP$ be $x$. Then, because $P$ HAS TO land on the segment $AB$, $x$ can basically be anywhere from $0$ to $10$, which consequently makes the total available length $10$.
>
> From here, recall that the area of a square with side length $AP$ is simply the formula $x^2$. Now, we write the inequality from the problem statement:
>
> $x^2 \le 16 \implies x \le 4$
>
> Essentially, this implies that $P$ must be chosen WITHIN the first $4$ units of the segment starting from the endpoint which is $A$. Lastly, the probability is the ratio of this valid length to the total length.
>
> $$\frac{4}{10} = \boxed{\frac{2}{5}}$$


## Worked Example #6 (The Tilted Cutting Line)
A point $P = (x,y)$ is chosen uniformly and randomly inside the following rectangle: rectangle $R = [0, 4] \times [0, 2]$. Your job is to find the probability that the coordinates of $P$ satisfy the inequality $x + 2y \le 4$.

> ### Solution
> We can find the probability by plotting the region defined by the inequality inside the boundaries of the rectangle in the coordinate plane.
>
> The total area of the rectangle is $\text{width} \times \text{height} = 4 \times 2 = 8$. 
>
> To find the valid region, we determine where the boundary line $x + 2y = 4$ intersects the edges of our rectangle:
> * Setting $y = 0$ gives $x = 4$, which is the bottom-right corner.
> * Setting $x = 0$ gives $y = 2$, which is the top-left corner.
>
> The line connects $(0,2)$ to $(4,0)$, slicing the rectangle exactly in half along its diagonal. The inequality $x + 2y \le 4$ represents the lower-left right triangle formed beneath this diagonal.
>
> Since the line bisects the rectangle perfectly, the area of this triangle is exactly half of the total area.
>
> $$\frac{4}{8} = \boxed{\frac{1}{2}}$$


## Worked Example #7 (Closer to the Center)
A point $P$ is chosen uniformly at random inside a square $S$ of side length $2$. Your job is to find the probability that $P$ is closer to the center of the square than it is to any of the four corners of the square.

> ### Solution
> To solve this problem, we need to find the geometric boundary where a point is perfectly equidistant from the center of the square and its vertices.
>
> The total area of the square is $2 \times 2 = 4$. The boundary lines where points are equally distant between the center and the corners are formed by the perpendicular bisectors of the segments connecting the center to each vertex.
>
> > Remember, the perpendicular bisector of a segment represents the exact locus of points equidistant from both endpoints.
>
> These boundary lines connect the midpoints of the four outer sides of the square, creating a smaller, tilted square diamond inside the main square. Connecting the midpoints of any square always partitions the space such that the inner diamond encloses exactly half of the total area.
>
> $$\frac{\text{Area}_{\text{diamond}}}{\text{Area}_{\text{total}}} = \boxed{\frac{1}{2}}$$

## Worked Example #8 (The Two-Stick Choice)
Two independent numbers $x$ and $y$ are chosen uniformly at random from the interval $[0, 2]$. Your job is to find the probability that $x \ge y + 1$.

> ### Solution
> We can model the independent choices of $x$ and $y$ as a two-dimensional region on a standard coordinate plane.
>
> Since both variables are chosen from the interval $[0, 2]$, the total sample space is represented by a $2 \times 2$ square with a total area of $2 \times 2 = 4$.
>
> We rewrite the inequality to graph it easily:
>
> $y \le x - 1$
>
> The boundary line $y = x - 1$ crosses the bottom edge of the square at $(1, 0)$ and the right edge of the square at $(2, 1)$. The inequality describes the region below this line, which forms a small right triangle in the bottom-right corner of the sample square.
>
> The base of this triangle along the $x$-axis is $2 - 1 = 1$, and its height along the right edge is $1 - 0 = 1$. We calculate the area of this triangle:
>
> $\text{Area}_{\text{triangle}} = \frac{1}{2} \times 1 \times 1 = \frac{1}{2}$
>
> The probability is the ratio of this triangular area to the total square area.
>
> $$\frac{1/2}{4} = \boxed{\frac{1}{8}}$$
