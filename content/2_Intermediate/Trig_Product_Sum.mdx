## Overview

Product-to-sum and sum-to-product formulas allow us to rewrite products of trigonometric functions as sums, and vice versa. They are especially useful when simplifying unusual angle expressions, proving identities, evaluating large trigonometric sums, etc.

All of the formulas follow directly from the angle addition formulas.

## Product-to-Sum Identities

$$
\sin\alpha\cos\beta = \tfrac12[\sin(\alpha+\beta)+\sin(\alpha-\beta)]
$$

$$
\cos\alpha\cos\beta = \tfrac12[\cos(\alpha+\beta)+\cos(\alpha-\beta)]
$$

$$
\sin\alpha\sin\beta = \tfrac12[\cos(\alpha-\beta)-\cos(\alpha+\beta)]
$$

*Proof:* We will prove the first identity; the rest follow similarly. Note that

$$
\sin(\alpha+\beta) = \sin(\alpha) \cos(\beta) + \sin(\beta) \cos(\alpha)
$$

$$
\sin(\alpha - \beta) = \sin(\alpha) \cos(-\beta) + \sin(-\beta) \cos(\alpha)
$$

and since $\cos(-x)=\cos(x)$ and $\sin(-x)=-\sin(x),$ we simplify the second equation:

$$
\sin(\alpha - \beta) = \sin(\alpha) \cos(\beta) - \sin(\beta) \cos(\alpha).
$$

Then adding the first and second equations, we obtain

$$
\sin(\alpha + \beta) + \sin(\alpha - \beta) = 2 \sin(\alpha) \cos(\beta).
$$

Divide by two and the identity follows.

**Don't overlook the "flipped order" on the $\sin\alpha\sin\beta$ formula!**


## Sum-to-Product Identities

$$
\sin A + \sin B = 2\sin\tfrac{A+B}{2}\cos\tfrac{A-B}{2}
$$

$$
\sin A - \sin B = 2\sin\tfrac{A-B}{2}\cos\tfrac{A+B}{2}
$$

$$
\cos A + \cos B = 2\cos\tfrac{A+B}{2}\cos\tfrac{A-B}{2}
$$

$$
\cos A - \cos B = -2\sin\tfrac{A+B}{2}\sin\tfrac{A-B}{2}
$$

*Proof:* 1, 3, and 4 follow from reverse substituting $\alpha = \tfrac{A+B}{2}$ and $\beta = \tfrac{A-B}{2}$ into the product-to-sum identities. 2 follows from 1, with the substitution $B \to -B.$

**Don't overlook the negative sign on the $\cos A - \cos B$ formula!**

If you have an expression of the form $\sin A + \cos B,$ consider turning it into one of these forms using $\cos(B) = \sin(90^\circ - B).$

## Common Uses

### Unusual Angles

Many AMC problems involve angles such as $15^\circ, 75^\circ,$ that do not have straightforward sine and cosine values. Product-to-sum or sum-to-product identities can convert expressions involving these angles into "nicer" expressions.

Notice that 

$$
\frac{75-15}{2} = 30,
$$
$$
\frac{75+15}{2} = 45,
$$
$$
75+15 = 90
$$
$$
75-15=60.
$$

The angles $30^\circ, 45^\circ, 60^\circ, 90^\circ$ have nice values of sine and cosine. So this can be extended to any two angles whose sum and/or difference is "nice," for instance, you should also consider using identities for expressions involving $105^\circ$ and $15^\circ,$ $10^\circ$ and $50^\circ,$ etc.

**Example:** Evaluate $\sin 10^\circ + \sin 50^\circ - \sin 70^\circ.$

**Solution:** Notice that

$$
\sin 10^\circ + \sin 50^\circ = 2 \sin 30^\circ \cos 20^\circ = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \cos 20^\circ = \cos 20^\circ.
$$

Then $\cos 20^\circ = \sin 70^\circ,$ so our answer is $0.$



## Worked Example

Simplify $\sin 75^\circ + \sin 15^\circ$.

$$
2\sin 45^\circ\cos 30^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}.
$$

## More Examples

### Example 1: Product to Sum

Simplify $\sin 20^\circ\cos 40^\circ$.

$\tfrac12[\sin 60^\circ+\sin(-20^\circ)] = \tfrac12(\sqrt{3}/2-\sin 20^\circ)$.

### Example 2: Sum to Product

Simplify $\cos 80^\circ + \cos 40^\circ$.

$2\cos 60^\circ\cos 20^\circ = \cos 20^\circ$.

### Example 3: Difference of Sines

Simplify $\sin 50^\circ-\sin 10^\circ$.

$2\cos 30^\circ\sin 20^\circ = \sqrt{3}\sin 20^\circ$.

## Strategy Checklist

- Identify whether a product or sum is present.
- Apply the correct formula and keep the $1/2$.
- Simplify with special angles where possible.

## Common Pitfalls

- Dropping the $1/2$ factor in product-to-sum.
- Mixing up signs, e.g. $A+B$ and $A-B$ halves. Note that $\cos(a-b) = \cos(a)\cos(b) + \sin(a)\sin(b),$ not minus!
- Switching sine and cosine formulas.
