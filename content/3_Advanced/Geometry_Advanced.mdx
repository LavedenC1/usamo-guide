## Overview

Advanced geometry blends similarity, power of a point, and homothety. Draw
accurate diagrams and track equal angles carefully.

## Key Ideas

- Ceva's theorem: for concurrent cevians, $\frac{AF}{FB}\cdot\frac{BD}{DC}\cdot\frac{CE}{EA}=1$.
- Homothety maps circles to circles and preserves tangency.
- Power of a point applies to tangents and secants.

## Core Skills

**Definition.** (Cevian) For a triangle $ABC$, a *cevian* is a line segment from one vertex to a point on the other side of the extension of the aforementioned side. For instance, if $D$ is a point on line $BC$, then $AD$ is a cevian.

### Ceva's Theorem

**Theorem.** (Ceva's Theorem) Let $ABC$ be a triangle. Suppose $D$, $E$, and $F$ are points on $BC$, $CA$, and $AB$, respectively. Then, 

$$\frac{AF}{FB}\cdot\frac{BD}{DC}\cdot\frac{CE}{EA}=1$$

if and only if $AD$, $BE$, and $CF$ are concurrent.

Now, we prove this in two parts!

**Part I.** ($AD$, $BE$, and $CF$ concur $\implies \prod = 1$) Suppose that $AD$, $BE$, and $CF$ concur at $X$. Then,

$$\dfrac{AF}{FB} = \dfrac{[AXC]}{[CXB]}, \quad \dfrac{BD}{DC} = \dfrac{[BXA]}{[AXC]}, \quad \dfrac{CE}{EA} = \dfrac{[CXB]}{[BXA]}.$$

Multiplying yields the desired result. For more description, $\dfrac{AF}{FB} = \dfrac{[AXC]}{[CXB]}$ holds as each triangle has the same base being $XC$ while the heights are proportional to the ratio $AF:FB$. $\blacksquare$

**Part II.** ($\prod = 1 \implies$ $AD$, $BE$, and $CF$ concur) Here, we assume $D$, $E$, and $F$ are points on their respective segments such that 

$$\frac{AF}{FB}\cdot\frac{BD}{DC}\cdot\frac{CE}{EA}=1.$$

Assume that $X = AD \cap BE$ and $CX \cap AB = F'$. Then, from Part I, 

$$\dfrac{AF'}{F'B} \cdot \dfrac{BD}{DC} \cdot \dfrac{CE}{EA} = 1.$$

Hence, $\dfrac{AF'}{F'B} = \dfrac{AF}{FB}$ which implies $F = F'$, as desired. $\blacksquare$

This is a really important property to not miss: you can use this to prove concurrency, which is a really subtle, but common property in lots of Olympiad Geometry problems, in particular!

### Menelaus' Theorem

**Theorem.** (Menelaus' Theorem) Let $ABC$ be a triangle. Points $P$, $Q$, and $R$ lie on $AB$, $BC$, and $CA$, respectively. Then,

$$\frac{AP}{PB}\cdot\frac{BQ}{QC}\cdot\frac{CF}{FA}=1$$

if and only if $AP$, $BQ$, and $CR$ are collinear.

The proof is fairly similar to Ceva's, so it can be an exercise for the reader!

Identify the homothety center to map tangency points and parallel lines.

### Combine with Similarity

Look for similar triangles created by tangents, secants, or parallel lines.

## Worked Example

If two tangents from $P$ touch a circle at $A$ and $B$, then $PA=PB$.
This follows from equal tangents to a circle.

## More Examples

### Example 1: Ceva

If $\frac{AF}{FB}=2$, $\frac{BD}{DC}=3$, find $\frac{CE}{EA}$ for concurrency.

$\frac{CE}{EA}=\frac{1}{6}$.

### Example 2: Power of a Point

From $P$, a secant meets the circle at $A,B$ with $PA=3$, $PB=12$. Find the
tangent length.

$PT^2=36$, so $PT=6$.

### Example 3: Homothety

If two circles are externally tangent at $T$, the line joining centers passes
through $T$. This is the homothety center.

## Strategy Checklist

- Decide if Ceva/Menelaus applies.
- Look for homothety centers to relate segments.
- Use power of a point to connect lengths.
