## Overview

At AIME level, inequalities need structure. You will often combine AM-GM with
clever substitutions or homogenization.

## Key Ideas

- Cauchy in Engel form: $\sum \frac{a^2}{b} \ge \frac{(a+b+c)^2}{b+c+a}$.
- Jensen applies to convex functions; start with simple cases.
- Look for symmetry and equality cases to guide algebra.

## Core Skills

### Homogenize

If an inequality is not homogeneous, scale variables to introduce a fixed sum
or product.

### Use Substitutions

Try $a=xy$, $b=yz$, $c=zx$ or $a=x+y$ to simplify symmetric expressions.

### Track Equality

Check when equality holds and use it to guide substitutions or case splits.

## Worked Example

For $a,b>0$, show $\frac{a^2}{b}+\frac{b^2}{a}\ge a+b$.

By Cauchy, $\frac{a^2}{b}+\frac{b^2}{a}\ge \frac{(a+b)^2}{a+b}=a+b$.

## More Examples

### Example 1: AM-GM Chain

For $a,b,c>0$, show $a+b+c \ge 3\sqrt[3]{abc}$.

Apply AM-GM to three variables.

### Example 2: Engel Form

Show $\sum \frac{a^2}{b} \ge \frac{(a+b+c)^2}{a+b+c}$.

Apply Engel form directly.

### Example 3: Symmetry

If $a+b+c=1$, find the minimum of $a^2+b^2+c^2$.

It is $1/3$ at $a=b=c=1/3$.

### Example 4: uvw Principle (very important! Must know for Tejs' Theorem in Level 4!)

Let $a,b,c>0$ and $a+b+c=3$. Your goal is show that the following expression is true:

$$
\frac1a+\frac1b+\frac1c \ge a^2+b^2+c^2+3.
$$

First, by the uvw principle, we can set $b=c=x$ and $a=3-2x$. Therefore, the inequality thus reduces to

$$
\frac{(x-1)^2(2x+1)(2x^2+2x+3)}{x(3-2x)} \ge 0,
$$

and the conclusion from here is immediate.

### Example 5: Engel and Cauchy

Your job is to show that

$$
\frac{(a+b)^2}{b+c}
+\frac{(b+c)^2}{c+a}
+\frac{(c+a)^2}{a+b}
\ge
2(a+b+c).
$$

We can simply expand the numerators and apply Engel form to the quadratic terms. Then, we can apply cauchy or nesbitt's inequalty to finish the problem!

### Example 6: Ravi Substitution (very important! Must know!)

Let $x,y,z$ be the side lengths of a triangle. Your goal is to show that

$$
\frac{x^2}{y+z-x}
+\frac{y^2}{z+x-y}
+\frac{z^2}{x+y-z}
\ge
x+y+z.
$$

We can use Ravi's substitution here:

$$
x=v+w,\quad y=w+u,\quad z=u+v.
$$

Thus, the inequality becomes:

$$
\sum \frac{(v+w)^2}{2u} \ge 2(u+v+w),
$$

and the conclusion simply follows from the Engel form.

Your job is to show that:

$$
(a+b+c)^4 \le 27(a^4+b^4+c^4).
$$

We can first apply HÃ¶lder's inequality:

$$
(a+b+c)^4
\le
(a^4+b^4+c^4)(1+1+1)^3.
$$

Now, since $(1+1+1)^3=27$, the result follows.

### Example 8: Jensen's Inequality (ALSO EXTREMELY IMPORTANT!)

Let $a,b,c>0$ with $a+b+c=1$. Your goal is to show that:

$$
\ln(1+a)+\ln(1+b)+\ln(1+c)
\le
3\ln\left(\frac43\right).
$$

First, notice that since $\ln(1+x)$ is concave, Jensen gives

$$
\frac{\sum \ln(1+a)}3
\le
\ln\left(1+\frac13\right).
$$

And multiplying this by $3$ gives the result

### Example 9: Muirhead (ALSO ALSO EXTREMELY IMPORTANT!)

Your goal is to show that:

$$
a^4+b^4+c^4+abc(a+b+c)
\ge
\sum_{\mathrm{sym}} a^3b.
$$

First, notice that since $(4,0,0)\succ(3,1,0)$, Muirhead gives

$$
3(a^4+b^4+c^4)
\ge
\sum_{\mathrm{sym}} a^3b.
$$

Then, we can combine it with

$$
3(a^4+b^4+c^4)\ge 4abc(a+b+c),
$$

and the result follows nicely :)

## Strategy Checklist

- Homogenize if needed.
- Use symmetry to reduce variables.
- Track equality conditions.
